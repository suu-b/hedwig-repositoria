# Exercise 2 — Feedback Loops

## Prompt

*Choose a real-world problem (social, environmental, or personal). Map out the feedback loops involved. Identify at least one reinforcing loop and one balancing loop. Propose an intervention that targets loop structure rather than individual elements.*

---

## Essay

Consider urban traffic congestion — a system that has resisted solution for decades precisely because every intervention aimed at the wrong level.

**Reinforcing Loop — Growth spiral:** More cars → more road infrastructure built → suburbs expand further → car dependency increases → more cars. Every highway expansion has historically induced demand rather than relieved it. The structure rewards itself.

**Balancing Loop — Cost signal:** Congestion → time cost rises → some drivers shift to transit or off-peak hours → congestion moderates. This loop *would* balance the system, but it operates too slowly and too weakly against the reinforcing dynamic.

**Where interventions fail:** Adding lanes feeds the reinforcing loop. Subsidising transit without removing road capacity leaves the reinforcing loop intact. The structure wins every time.

**Structural intervention:** Congestion pricing with dynamic rates targets the *delay* in the balancing loop. It sharpens and speeds up the cost signal, strengthening the balancing loop without requiring anyone to be altruistic or rational in an abstract sense. The system is made to think through price rather than through moral persuasion.

**The broader point:** Most policy failure is not intellectual failure. It is a failure to see that you are fighting a loop, not a problem.
